# 06 — Deep Dive: Strategy & Direction

*"Where am I going?" — the disciplines that set the course and adapt when reality changes.*

Covers: Strategic Planning, Business Development, Innovation / R&D, Change Management, Goal-Setting

---

## Why Strategy Matters Even for a One-Person Business

"Strategy is for big companies." Wrong. A solo builder making strategic decisions every day — they're just not calling it strategy:

- "Should I focus on garden offices or traditional sheds?"
- "Should I try to serve a wider area or stay local?"
- "Is it worth investing in this configurator when I could be building?"
- "Should I hire someone or stay solo?"

These are all strategic decisions. The difference between a business that grows and one that stays stuck is whether these decisions are intentional or accidental.

## 1. Strategic Planning

### What It Is
Defining where you want to be in 1-3-5 years and figuring out how to get there.

### Strategic Planning Frameworks

| Framework | What It Does | Complexity | Fit for Small Business |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **SWOT Analysis** | Maps Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats in a 2×2 grid | Simple | **Excellent.** Quick, visual, everyone understands it. |
| **Business Model Canvas** | 9 building blocks: value proposition, customers, channels, revenue, resources, activities, partners, costs | Medium | **Very good.** Forces you to think about the whole business on one page. |
| **Porter's Five Forces** | Analyses competitive pressure: rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power | Medium | **Good for understanding market.** Who else builds sheds? What's the threat from flat-pack? |
| **PESTLE Analysis** | Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental factors | Medium | **Useful occasionally.** Planning permission changes? Timber tariffs? AI impact? |
| **Ansoff Matrix** | Growth strategy: existing/new products × existing/new markets | Simple | **Good for growth decisions.** New building types? New geographies? |
| **Balanced Scorecard** | Strategy across 4 perspectives: financial, customer, process, learning | Complex | **Overkill as formal system.** But the 4 perspectives are useful thinking tools. |
| **OKRs** | Objectives (what) + Key Results (measurable how) per quarter | Medium | **Excellent.** Simple, focused, forces measurability. |

### What "Strategy" Actually Looks Like for a Builder

Forget 50-page strategy documents. For Andrew, strategy is answering these questions, reviewing them quarterly:

**Vision:** What does this business look like in 3 years?
- "I want to be the premium bespoke garden building company in [region], known for Douglas fir craftsmanship and a unique 3D design experience."

**Objectives (this year):**
- Revenue target: £X
- Number of builds: Y
- Configurator monthly visitors: Z
- Launch Forge AI templates: 1 by Q3

**Key decisions to make:**
- Hire a labourer or stay solo?
- Invest in a bigger workshop?
- Expand geographic range?
- Add new building types (garden offices, studios)?

**What's working / what's not:**
- Configurator brings enquiries but conversion rate is unknown
- Douglas fir quality is a differentiator — competitors don't offer it
- No proper quoting system — quotes take too long to produce

### The Strategy Review Cycle

```
Quarterly:
  1. Review objectives — on track?
  2. What's changed in the market?
  3. What did we learn this quarter?
  4. Adjust plan for next quarter

Annually:
  1. Full strategy review
  2. Set new objectives
  3. Review business model — still right?
  4. Investment decisions
```

## 2. Business Development

### What It Is
Identifying and pursuing growth opportunities — new markets, new products, new partnerships, new capabilities.

### Business Development for a Garden Building Business

| Growth Direction | Example | Risk | Reward |
|-----------------|---------|------|--------|
| **More of the same** | Build more sheds in the same area | Low | Steady growth, capacity-limited |
| **New product types** | Garden offices, studios, cabins, gazebos, annexes | Medium | Larger market, higher prices |
| **New geography** | Serve a wider radius, or specific affluent areas | Medium | More customers, more travel cost |
| **New channel** | Sell configurator access to other builders | Medium-High | Recurring revenue, different business model |
| **New business** | Forge AI templates, coaching | High | Scalable, but different skill set |
| **Partnerships** | Landscape architects, property developers, garden centres | Low-Medium | Referral pipeline, credibility |

### The Business Development Pipeline

Not every idea should be pursued. A simple pipeline:

```
IDEA → RESEARCH → EVALUATE → PILOT → SCALE (or KILL)
```

This maps directly to the Incubator in the Solution Planner:
- **Concept** = Idea + Research
- **Developing** = Evaluate + Pilot
- **Ready to Graduate** = Proven, ready to Scale

## 3. Innovation / R&D

### What It Is
Creating new products, improving processes, adopting new technology, and experimenting.

### R&D in Andrew's Business

The configurator IS the R&D. It's also the competitive moat:

| Innovation | Status | Impact |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| **3D parametric configurator** | Built, evolving | Unique in UK market — no competitor has this |
| **Automated cutting lists / BOM** | Built into configurator | Direct cost savings, faster quoting |
| **AI assistant (James)** | Operational | Business management, research, development — one person + AI = team |
| **Animation / video pipeline** | Built | Marketing asset generation |
| **Business OS templates** | In development (this project) | Forge AI product, revenue stream |

### R&D Tax Credits (UK)

Andrew's configurator development may qualify for **R&D tax relief**:
- **SME R&D Relief**: Deduct up to 186% of qualifying R&D expenditure (from April 2024: merged scheme)
- **Qualifying activities**: Creating new technology, process innovation, overcoming technical uncertainty
- The parametric configurator arguably qualifies — it's a novel technical solution solving a genuine technical challenge (real-time parametric 3D with accurate construction data)

**The system should track R&D-eligible time and expenditure separately** — even rough time logs help with claims.

## 4. Change Management

### What It Is
Managing the human and organisational side of change — adopting new tools, processes, or strategies.

### Why It Matters Here

Andrew is essentially one person undergoing massive change:
- From manual quoting → parametric quoting
- From no business system → universal business OS
- From solo builder → potentially building a team
- From one business → multiple businesses (garden buildings + Forge AI + Whelpley Farm)

### Principles for Small-Business Change

1. **Change one thing at a time.** Don't try to implement CRM + job costing + scheduling + marketing automation simultaneously.
2. **Start with what hurts most.** What's the biggest pain point right now? Fix that first.
3. **Make the new way easier than the old way.** If the system is harder to use than a notebook, Andrew won't use it.
4. **Quick wins build momentum.** Get something useful working in week 1, even if it's simple.
5. **Don't require double-entry.** If Andrew has to enter data in the configurator AND in a CRM AND in a spreadsheet, the system has failed.

## 5. Goal-Setting Frameworks

### OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

Developed at Intel, adopted by Google. Simple and effective:

**Objective:** Increase garden buildings revenue by 50%
**Key Results:**
- KR1: Achieve 20 completed builds this year (vs 13 last year)
- KR2: Increase average order value to £8,000 (from £6,000)
- KR3: Configurator generates 5 qualified leads per month

**Objective:** Launch Forge AI business template product
**Key Results:**
- KR1: Complete business OS research (this work)
- KR2: Build Solution Planner v2 with template system
- KR3: Package first template (construction/trades) by Q3
- KR4: 3 beta users testing the template by Q4

### The Key Difference: Outcomes, Not Tasks

Goals should be about **outcomes** (what changed in the world), not **outputs** (what you did):
- ❌ "Build a CRM" (output)
- ✅ "Never lose a lead due to forgotten follow-up" (outcome)
- ❌ "Create a financial dashboard" (output)
- ✅ "Know the exact profit on every job within 24 hours of completion" (outcome)

## What This Means for Our Universal Business OS

### Key Insights

1. **Strategy in the system should be lightweight, not academic.** No 50-page strategy documents. A simple page with: Vision, This Year's Objectives (3-5), Key Results per objective, Quarterly Review notes. That's it.

2. **OKRs map naturally to the Solution Planner.** The "Today" view should show: What are this quarter's objectives? How are the key results tracking? What needs attention?

3. **The Incubator IS the business development pipeline.** We already built the 3-stage incubator (Concept → Developing → Ready to Graduate). This is exactly the right pattern for managing growth opportunities.

4. **Business Model Canvas should be a template.** Each business instance (garden buildings, Whelpley Farm, Forge AI) should be able to fill in a Business Model Canvas. This forces clarity about what each business actually does.

5. **Change management is a UX problem.** If we design the system right — easy, joyful, invisible CRM, one data entry → many views — the change management problem mostly solves itself. The best change management is making the new way obviously better.

6. **R&D tracking is a hidden revenue opportunity.** If the system can track development time and categorise it as R&D-eligible, Andrew might be able to claim tax relief he's currently missing.

### Architecture Principle: Strategy as the Top-Level Lens

```
STRATEGY (Where are we going?)
    │
    ├── Vision / Mission
    │
    ├── OKRs (This Quarter)
    │       │
    │       ├── KR1 → driven by SALES data (leads, conversions)
    │       ├── KR2 → driven by FINANCIAL data (revenue, margins)
    │       └── KR3 → driven by OPERATIONS data (builds completed)
    │
    ├── Business Model Canvas (per business)
    │
    ├── Incubator (growth opportunities pipeline)
    │
    └── Quarterly Review (what's working, what's not)
```

Strategy is the lens through which all other data becomes meaningful. Revenue is just a number until you compare it to an objective. Leads are just contacts until you measure them against a key result. The Solution Planner's "Today" view should be strategy-driven: "Here's what matters today, in the context of where you're trying to go."
